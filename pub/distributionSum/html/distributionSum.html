I am trying to prove $$ \begin{align} \sigma \sum_{i=1}^K \epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 K), \label{_auto1} \end{align} $$

when \( \epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1) \).

I get $$ \begin{align} \mathrm{Var} (\sigma \sum_{i=1}^K \epsilon_i) &= \sigma^2 \mathrm{Var} (\sum_{i=1}^K \epsilon_i) \label{_auto2}\\ &=\sigma^2 \sum_{i=1}^K \mathrm{Var}(\epsilon_i) \label{_auto3}\\ &=\sigma^2 K \mathrm{Var}(\epsilon_i) \label{_auto4}\\ &=\sigma^2 K \label{_auto5} \end{align} $$

Question: 1) The switch of the sum and the variance is only possible when the \( \epsilon \)'s are independent?
